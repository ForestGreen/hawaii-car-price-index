Car Insurance in Hawaii: Rates, Requirements & Best Providers

Your complete guide to Hawaii auto insurance costs, legal requirements, and strategies to save money on premiums across all islands.

Introduction: Why Car Insurance is Different in Hawaii

Purchasing car insurance in Hawaii comes with unique challenges that mainland drivers often don't face. Island living means higher vehicle replacement costs, unique weather and volcanic hazards, aggressive traffic patterns on Oahu, and insurance regulations specifically designed for Hawaii's isolated market.

Whether you're a long-time resident or moving to the islands, understanding Hawaii's insurance landscape can save you hundreds of dollars annually while ensuring proper protection. This guide covers minimum legal requirements, average costs by island and vehicle type, the state's unique no-fault system, and practical strategies for finding the best rates.

Key Fact

Hawaii is one of only a handful of states that require Personal Injury Protection (PIP) coverage. This no-fault coverage is mandatory and covers medical expenses regardless of who caused an accident—a critical protection in Hawaii's insurance system.

Hawaii Minimum Car Insurance Requirements

Hawaii law sets specific minimum insurance requirements that every vehicle owner must carry. Understanding these requirements is critical—driving without insurance can result in suspended license, fines up to $1,000, and vehicle impoundment.

Minimum Liability Coverage

Hawaii requires all drivers to carry liability insurance at these minimum limits:

  • $20,000 per person for bodily injury liability
  • $40,000 per accident for bodily injury liability
  • $10,000 for property damage liability

These limits cover injuries and property damage you cause to others. These are among the lowest in the nation, and most insurance experts recommend carrying higher limits—a $100,000/$300,000/$100,000 policy provides better protection for a modest premium increase.

Personal Injury Protection (PIP) — Hawaii's Unique Requirement

Hawaii uniquely requires Personal Injury Protection (PIP), a form of no-fault coverage. Minimum PIP coverage is $10,000, which covers:

  • Medical expenses and hospital bills
  • Lost wages while you recover
  • Essential services (childcare, housekeeping) you cannot perform
  • Death benefits (up to $5,000 for funeral expenses)

Unlike mainland states, PIP pays these benefits regardless of fault. After an accident, your PIP coverage pays first, making it a critical protection for Hawaii drivers and passengers.

Understanding Hawaii's No-Fault Insurance System

Hawaii operates under a "no-fault" insurance system, which fundamentally differs from liability-based states. Here's how it works:

  • After an accident, your own insurance covers your injuries and losses, regardless of who caused the accident
  • You cannot sue the at-fault driver unless your medical expenses exceed your PIP coverage limits or you suffer permanent scarring/disfigurement
  • This system theoretically reduces insurance costs and litigation by making recovery faster and more predictable
  • You must still carry liability insurance to cover damages you cause to others

The no-fault system protects you as a driver but also prevents you from collecting pain-and-suffering damages from the at-fault driver in minor accident cases. For major accidents, you can pursue additional damages if your injuries are severe enough.

Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage

Hawaii also requires uninsured motorist (UM) coverage at the same limits as your liability coverage. This protects you if hit by a driver with insufficient or no insurance. Underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage is optional but highly recommended—it covers damages when the at-fault driver's insurance is insufficient.

Hawaii has a significant uninsured motorist problem, with studies suggesting 10-15% of drivers are uninsured. Carrying UM/UIM coverage protects your family if you're hit by an uninsured driver.

7 Proven Ways to Save on Hawaii Car Insurance

Hawaii car insurance doesn't have to be expensive. Here are practical strategies to reduce your premiums while maintaining adequate protection:

1. Bundle Your Policies (20–25% Savings)

Combining auto, home, renters, or life insurance with one company typically saves 20–25% on each policy. State Farm and Progressive offer strong bundling discounts. If you own or rent in Hawaii, bundling is one of the fastest ways to reduce your overall insurance costs.

2. Maintain a Good Driving Record (5–15% Savings)

A clean driving record is your most valuable discount. Most insurers offer 5–15% discounts for 3+ years without accidents or violations. If you've had a violation, one clean year can often result in getting back some discounts. Defensive driving courses can also restore discounts and reduce points on your license.

3. Install Anti-Theft and Safety Devices (5–15% Savings)

Modern anti-theft systems, GPS trackers, and alarm systems reduce theft risk and can earn you 5–15% discounts. Hawaii has higher-than-average auto theft on some islands, making these devices particularly valuable. Ask your insurer which devices qualify for discounts.

4. Leverage Military Discounts (10–25% Savings)

Active-duty military, veterans, military retirees, and military families can receive 10–25% discounts through USAA, State Farm, Geico, and other providers. Hawaii's large military population makes this one of the largest available discounts. Even retirees with military ID may qualify.

5. Use Usage-Based Insurance Programs (10–30% Savings)

Progressive's Snapshot and similar programs monitor your driving habits and reward safe driving with discounts up to 30%. If you drive safely, predictably, and mainly during safe hours, usage-based insurance can significantly reduce your premiums. These programs use smartphone apps or plug-in devices.

6. Increase Your Deductible (10–30% Savings)

Raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 or higher can reduce comprehensive and collision premiums by 10–30%. This works best if you have emergency savings—you'll pay the higher deductible in an accident. Calculate whether your premium savings exceed your additional out-of-pocket risk.

7. Shop Around and Compare Quotes (30–50% Potential Savings)

Insurance companies use different rating formulas and factors. Getting quotes from 3–5 companies can reveal savings of 30–50%. Use online quote tools but also contact local agents, especially GEICO and State Farm, for personalized quotes. Rates change regularly, so shopping every 2–3 years is smart.

Pro Tip: Timing Matters

When moving to Hawaii or purchasing a car, get insurance quotes before finalizing the purchase. The car's make, model, year, and safety features significantly impact rates. Some cars are cheaper to insure by $30–50/month—choosing the right vehicle can pay off over years of ownership.

Hawaii-Specific Insurance Considerations

Hawaii's unique geography, climate, and isolation create insurance considerations you won't find on the mainland. Understanding these will help you choose appropriate coverage.

No-Fault Insurance Explained

We touched on this earlier, but it's worth deepening. Hawaii's no-fault system means your insurance pays your claims first, regardless of who caused the accident. Key implications:

  • Your PIP coverage pays medical bills immediately—you don't wait for the at-fault party's insurance to settle liability
  • You recover faster because there's no battle over liability and fault
  • You can only sue the at-fault driver if your damages exceed your PIP limits or you suffered serious permanent injury
  • This protects good drivers but can prevent you from recovering pain-and-suffering damages in minor accidents

The no-fault system is designed to reduce litigation and speed recovery. If you're hit by another driver, your own insurance pays, creating a faster process—but you may have fewer options for recovering additional damages in minor accidents.

Personal Injury Protection (PIP) Coverage Details

Hawaii's mandatory PIP (minimum $10,000) covers:

  • Medical expenses: Doctor visits, hospital stays, surgery, physical therapy, prescription drugs
  • Lost wages: Income you lose while unable to work (typically 80% of lost wages, capped at a limit)
  • Essential services: Costs of services you normally perform but can't while injured (childcare, housekeeping, etc.)
  • Death benefits: Up to $5,000 for funeral and burial expenses if you die from accident injuries

PIP limits can be higher than minimums—many drivers choose $25,000 or $50,000 for better protection. Higher PIP limits are particularly valuable if you have dependents or significant medical history.

Uninsured Motorist Coverage (Critical in Hawaii)

Hawaii requires UM coverage equal to your liability limits. This covers you if hit by a driver with no insurance or insufficient coverage. Hawaii has a significant uninsured driver problem—studies suggest 10–15% of drivers are uninsured.

Recommended action: Don't accept Hawaii's legal minimum UM coverage. Choose higher UM limits (matching or exceeding your liability limits) for better protection. Many insurers recommend $100,000 UM coverage for Hawaii drivers.

Volcanic/Lava Damage Coverage

Hawaii's active volcanos present unique risks, particularly on the Big Island. Standard comprehensive coverage covers damage from lava flows, earthquakes, and related volcanic activity. However:

  • Comprehensive coverage is only required if you finance your vehicle
  • If you own your car outright, comprehensive is optional but recommended on the Big Island
  • Lava damage claims can exceed car value—ensure your coverage limits are adequate
  • Some older policies may exclude volcanic damage, so review your policy if you live near volcanic zones

If you live in a lava-risk area (Big Island, particularly lower Puna district), comprehensive coverage isn't just recommended—it's essential.

Flood and Tsunami Coverage

Hawaii's island location means flood and tsunami risks are higher than mainland states. Standard auto insurance typically covers

flood damage through comprehensive coverage. However, specific concerns include:

  • Coastal areas on all islands have tsunami flood potential
  • Heavy rainfall and tropical storms can cause flooding in low-lying areas
  • Comprehensive coverage covers flood damage—don't skip it if you live in a flood zone
  • Check your deductible; $1,000 deductibles mean you pay that amount out-of-pocket for flood claims

Salt Air and Environmental Damage

Hawaii's salt air environment is particularly harsh on vehicles. While comprehensive coverage doesn't specifically cover oxidation and rust from salt air, it does cover damage when these issues lead to mechanical failure. Strategies to protect your vehicle:

  • Wash your car weekly or biweekly, especially if you live near the ocean
  • Park in covered/garage parking when possible
  • Maintain your vehicle's paint and undercarriage regularly
  • Comprehensive coverage protects against total loss from environmental damage

Salt air damage accumulates over years. Many used cars imported to Hawaii have significant hidden rust and corrosion—this is why obtaining a pre-purchase inspection is critical. See our Best Used Cars in Hawaii guide for detailed inspection tips.

Moving to Hawaii? Your Insurance Transfer Guide

If you're relocating from the mainland to Hawaii, updating your car insurance is a critical task. Here's how to handle it:

Step 1: Notify Your Current Insurer (Within 30 Days)

Contact your current insurance company and inform them you're moving to Hawaii. Most policies provide temporary coverage (often 5–14 days) when you change residency. Use this window to secure a Hawaii policy, but don't let coverage lapse—driving without insurance in Hawaii carries severe penalties.

Step 2: Understand Rate Increases

Moving to Hawaii will likely increase your insurance costs, particularly if you're coming from a rural or less dense area. Expect rates to increase 15–35% depending on:

  • Your origin state's rates vs. Hawaii's higher premiums
  • Which island you're moving to (Oahu rates higher than Big Island)
  • Your driving record and age
  • Vehicle type and value

Don't be surprised by rate jumps—this is normal for mainland-to-Hawaii moves. However, your driving record and good credit still earn discounts, so emphasize those when getting quotes.

Step 3: Special Hawaii Requirements

Ensure any Hawaii policy includes Hawaii's unique requirements:

  • Personal Injury Protection (PIP) - $10,000 minimum required
  • Uninsured Motorist Coverage - equal to your liability limits
  • Proper liability limits ($20,000/$40,000/$10,000 minimum)

Some national insurers' mainland policies don't automatically include Hawaii-compliant PIP. Explicitly confirm your new Hawaii policy includes all required coverage.

Step 4: Vehicle Registration and Insurance Coordination

You'll need to register your vehicle in Hawaii within 30 days of establishing residency. Proof of insurance is required for registration. Coordinate these two processes:

  1. Get Hawaii insurance quotes and select a policy
  2. Purchase the insurance (effective date can be set for your move date)
  3. Use your insurance proof of financial responsibility for vehicle registration
  4. Register your vehicle with Hawaii Department of Transportation

Vehicle registration and car insurance must overlap—gaps in coverage are violations. Plan for a seamless transition.

Step 5: Used Car Insurance and Value Verification

If you're bringing a used vehicle to Hawaii, get it appraised before moving. Hawaii's used car market has different values than the mainland—your vehicle's insurance value may be higher or lower than expected. A pre-purchase inspection and market assessment (see our Why Cars Cost More in Hawaii guide) helps ensure your insurance coverage is appropriate.

Documentation You'll Need

When transferring insurance to Hawaii, have your driver's license, vehicle identification number (VIN), current insurance information, and proof of residency (lease agreement, utility bill, etc.) ready. Some insurers may ask for a Hawaii driver's license, but moving companies and utility connections can serve as proof of residency initially.

Frequently Asked Questions About Hawaii Car Insurance

What is the minimum car insurance required in Hawaii?

Hawaii requires minimum liability coverage of $20,000 per person/$40,000 per accident for bodily injury and $10,000 for property damage. Hawaii also uniquely requires Personal Injury Protection (PIP) coverage at a minimum of $10,000, which covers medical expenses regardless of fault. Uninsured motorist coverage equal to your liability limits is also required.

Is Hawaii a no-fault insurance state?

Yes, Hawaii is a no-fault insurance state. After an accident, your own insurance covers your injuries and losses regardless of who caused it. You can only sue the at-fault driver if your damages exceed your PIP coverage limits or you suffered permanent scarring/disfigurement. This system prioritizes fast recovery but limits ability to recover pain-and-suffering damages in minor accidents.

What is PIP coverage and why does Hawaii require it?

Personal Injury Protection (PIP) is no-fault coverage that pays for medical expenses, lost wages, essential services, and death benefits after an accident—regardless of who caused it. Hawaii requires it to protect drivers and passengers without requiring proof of fault. This unique feature makes recovery faster and more predictable, though it limits some legal remedies available in other states.

How much does car insurance cost in Hawaii?

Average costs vary by coverage level and island. Minimum coverage averages $650–$950 annually; standard coverage averages $1,050–$1,450 annually; full coverage averages $1,600–$2,300 annually. Oahu has higher costs ($1,250–$1,550) due to traffic density, while the Big Island and Kauai are lower ($950–$1,250). Rates also vary by vehicle type, driver age, record, and other factors.

Which islands have the highest car insurance rates?

Oahu consistently has the highest rates due to dense traffic on Honolulu streets, higher accident frequency, and higher vehicle values. Maui has moderate rates. The Big Island and Kauai have the lowest rates due to lighter traffic and fewer accidents. If insurance costs are a concern, living on a neighbor island can result in 10–20% savings compared to Oahu.

What discounts can I get on Hawaii car insurance?

Common discounts include multi-policy bundling (20–25%), good driver discounts (5–10%), anti-theft device discounts (5–15%), military discounts (10–20% for active duty and military families), and usage-based insurance programs. Some insurers also offer discounts for defensive driving courses, low mileage, and paying your premium in full. Shop around—different insurers offer different discount combinations.

Do I need comprehensive coverage in Hawaii?

If you finance or lease a vehicle, comprehensive coverage is required by your lender. If you own outright, it's optional but highly recommended in Hawaii due to volcanic risks (Big Island), flood/tsunami potential, salt air damage, and higher theft rates. Comprehensive coverage is typically inexpensive and protects against total loss from environmental hazards unique to Hawaii.

Can I transfer my mainland car insurance to Hawaii?

Your existing policy provides temporary coverage (often 5–14 days) when you move, but you must update your address to Hawaii within 30 days. Your rates will likely increase due to Hawaii's higher insurance costs and unique requirements like mandatory PIP coverage. You'll need a new Hawaii policy that meets state requirements. Contact your insurer immediately when moving to Hawaii to secure proper coverage.

What should I do if I'm hit by an uninsured driver in Hawaii?

This is why uninsured motorist (UM) coverage is critical in Hawaii. Your UM coverage (required to equal your liability limits) pays for your injuries and vehicle damage. Call police and report the accident. Document everything—photos, witness contact information, driver details if available. Your insurance will investigate and pay through your UM coverage. Higher UM limits (beyond the legal minimum) are recommended for better protection.

Is auto insurance more expensive if I move to Hawaii from the mainland?

Yes, almost always. Hawaii's rates are 15–35% higher than many mainland states due to higher vehicle replacement costs, unique coverage requirements, limited competition among insurers, and higher accident frequency on some islands. Oahu rates are especially high. However, if moving from a major metropolitan area to the Big Island or Kauai, costs may be comparable or slightly lower.
